# CS_230_The_Gaming_Room
 Software design document used to design a web-based version their Android-based game Draw It or Lose It.

Briefly summarize The Gaming Room client and their software requirements. Who was the client? What type of software did they want you to design?
> In an effort to reach more customers, social media company Chat Away wants to develop a mobile platform version of their website. This app needs to be distributable in both Android and iPhone app stores. Budget and timeline remain a high priority within the project.

What did you do particularly well in developing this documentation?
> I think I did well in the evaluation section of the document, listing out positives and negatives of each section in the table.

What about the process of working through a design document did you find helpful when developing the code?
> Some amount of discovery about a project is always helpful. That is, lay out the potential directions and then try to describe what each adds or subtracts from the project. I think the difficult part is knowing when you have done enough exploration.

If you could choose one part of your work on these documents to revise, what would you pick? How would you improve it?
> I think I could have done better describing the domain model in a more streamlined way. To me in some sections, my wording might have been a little confusing, especially to those with no background in programming.  

How did you interpret the user’s needs and implement them into your software design? Why is it so important to consider the user’s needs when designing?
> If you are not addressing your user's needs they will abandon your application as soon as there is a viable alternative. Moreover, continually thrilling your users is also paramount to retaining even the most loyal user.

How did you approach designing software? What techniques or strategies would you use in the future to analyze and design a similar software application?
> My approach to software design centers on decoupling complexity through a rigorous "requirements-first" analysis, ensuring the architecture solves the actual business problem rather than just providing a technical playground. In the future, I would prioritize an API-first, modular strategy—utilizing established design patterns like SOLID and Hexagonal Architecture—to ensure the system remains maintainable and scalable. By defining clear boundaries between entities and implementing automated testing and observability from the outset, the design becomes a living framework that can adapt to changing user needs without collapsing under technical debt.
